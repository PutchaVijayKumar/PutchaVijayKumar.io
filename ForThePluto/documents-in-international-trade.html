Documents used in International Trade


The documents used in international trade can be divided into the following two categories.
  1. Commercial Documents; and
  2. Financial Documents

Commercial Documents

There are many commercial documents that are used in international trade. The type and number of documents depend from trade to trade. The following are some of the commercial documents.
  1. Commercial Invoice
  2. Packing List
  3. Weight Certificate
  4. Insurance Documents
  5. Open Policy
  6. Insurance Certificate
  7. Certificate of Origin
  8. Inspection Certificate
  9. Transport Documents
    1. Bill of Lading
    2. Multimodal Transport Documents
    3. Road / Rail / Inland Waterway Bill
    4. Airway Bill / Air Consignment Note
    5. Courier and Post Receipts

Financial Documents

In general, the following are the two commonly used financial documents used in International Trade.
  1. Bill of Exchange
  2. Promisory Note


Commercial Documents

The following are the features of various commercial documents.

Commercial Invoice

  • It is issued by the seller
  • It has the following contents:
    • The value of the goods i.e. the unit price and the total quantity
    • The terms of the sale
    • Description of goods covered by the shipment
    • Details of any additional charges i.e. insurance freight costs, etc.
  • The purpose of the Invoice is to evidence the sale of goods in terms required by credit.

Packaging List

  • Typically, issued by seller
  • It contains the list of goods supplied with weights, marks and other details
  • The purpose of this document is to assist the buyer, clearing agents and customs in knowing what and how the goods have been packed, and to enable spot checks and content of packages.

Weight Certificate

  • Typically, it is issued by independent or official organisation.
  • It contains the gross and net weight of merchandise
  • The purpose of this document is to satisfy the applicant, clearing agents and carriers about weight of goods

Insurance Documents

  • The two major types of insurance documents are (1) Insurance Policy; and (2) Insurance Certificate
  • The insurance policy gives full details of the terms and conditions of the insurance, and also provides a valid evidence of contract of insurance to court
  • The insurance certificate gives brief details of the terms and conditions of the insurance arrangement. It may not always be used to prove contract of insurance in court, unless with the Open Policy.

Open Policy

  • When a trader imports or exports goods on a regular basis, he can take out an Open Policy which covers all shipments upto a ceiling limit under the same clauses and warranties.
  • Once an Open Policy has been accepted, for each shipment the insured is automatically insured against the risks specified under the Open Policy. If anything happens, he will be reimbursed up to the amount agreed per shipment.
  • Insuring under the Open Policy arrangement therefore means that insurance does not have to be arranged each time a shipment is made.

Insurance Certificate

  • It is a signed certificate
  • It is issued by an insurance company / underwriter or its agent
  • It is transferable by endorsement
  • The amount of insurance cover is generally 110% of gross invoice / payment / acceptance / negotiation value.
  • The amount of insurance and its currency should be the same as sale price under export contract
  • The insurance cover to be effective from the date of loading / dispatch / taking in-charge
  • The contents of this document usually are amount of insurance, description of goods, date of effective cover and validity and risks covered

Certificate of Origin

  • It is typically issued by a Chamber of Commerce or Supplier
  • It contains details such as certificate of origin, description of goods and consignor details.
  • The purpose of the document is to enable the importer to receive the benefit of reduction of import duties and to satisfy customs and import requirements in country of buyer.

Inspection Certificate

  • Typically issued by independent agency, manufacturer or seller
  • It contains details as stipulated in credit, and other details relating to results of specified tests, inspection of goods, etc.
  • The purpose of this document is to satisfy applicant and end-buyer import control authorities about quality of goods.

Transport Document

  • There are many types of transport documents such as bill of lading, airway bill, multimodal transport document, road/rail inward, waterway bill, courier / postal receipts, etc. The following provides a brief of these documents.

    • Bill of Lading

    • It is a signed receipt, normally issued on or behalf of a carrier.
    • It is a transferable document of title
    • The contains the following:
      • Terms and conditions of carriage
      • Evidence of goods on board
      • Name of vessel carrying goods
      • Requisite authentication
    • The purpose of this document is to serve as a contract for delivery of merchandise to a person at specified location.
    • There are three types of Bill of Lading, as follows:

      • Order Bill of Lading
      • Straight Bill of Lading
      • Charter Party Bill of Lading

    Multimodal Transport Document

    • Covers at least two modes of transport
    • Signed receipt
    • Issued by Multimodal Transport Operator (MTO)
    • Transferable document of title (subject to previous defects in title)
    • May help reduce transit times
    • Reduce costs
    • Contains
      • Terms and conditions of carriage
      • Evidence of dispatch / goods on board taken in-charge
      • Name of carrier
      • Requisite authentication
    • The purpose of this document is to provide a convenient method of sending goods from beneficiary to applicant premises using a sample document.

    Road / Rail / Inland Waterway Bill

    • Signed receipt
    • Issued by road / rail / inland waterway transporters
    • Non - negotiable document
    • It contains terms and conditions of carriage, evidence of dispatch / goods on board / taken in-charge, name of the carrier, requisite authentication
    • The purpose of this document is to provide evidence of shipment and/or delivery of goods for shipment by road / rail / inland waterway.

    Air Waybill / Air Consignment Note

    • It is a signed receipt issued by air carrier or freight forwarder
    • It is non-negotiable
    • It contians terms and conditions of carriage, evidence of acceptance for carriage, name of vessel carrying goods, requisite authentication
    • The purpose of this document is to evidence shipment and/or delivery of goods for shipment, etc.

    Courier and Post Receipts

    • It is issued by courier or post office
    • It is a non-negotiable document
    • It is not a document of title
    • It contains requisite authentication, evidence of pick-up or receipt, date of shipment and consignee details
    • The purpose of the document is to evidence that goods have been received by the courier / post office and delivery is to the named addressee.


Financial Documents

The following are the features of various financial documents.

Bill of Exchange

  • It is an order in writing
  • It is signed by the drawer (seller)
  • It requests the drawer (buyer) to make payment in lawful money
  • The payment can be made immediately or at a determinable future time
  • The payment is made to the order of a third party (payee)

Promissory Note

  • A written, dated and signed two-party instrument containing an unconditional promise by the maker to pay a definte sum of money to a payee on demand or at a specified future date.
  • The only difference between a promissory note and a bill of exchange is that the maker of the note pays the payee personally, rather than ordering a third party to do so.
